What it does

Two independent sensors, one signal

Members of the U.S. Congress are required by law to disclose their stock trades — and some of them are suspiciously good at it. Separately, when institutional money moves, it leaves footprints: abnormally large trades that stand out from everyday retail churn. Ahab watches both, every night, and cross-references them.

SENSOR 01 — DISCLOSURES

The congressional radar

Downloads every STOCK Act disclosure back to 2012 (~57,000 trades), computes risk-adjusted returns per politician, and assigns each a tier from S (elite) to F. When multiple high-tier officials buy the same stock within 30 days, that convergence gets flagged and ranked.

SENSOR 02 — LIVE FLOW

The whale sonar

Scans stocks and crypto for concentrated order flow — single enormous prints against a sea of small retail trades. Separates "whale" footprints from crowd noise, tracks accumulation vs. distribution per symbol, and reads the market regime from index-level flow.

SENSOR 03 — SYNTHESIS

The interpreter

An LLM reads the night's volume anomalies, calibration history, and news handoff, then writes the morning report in plain English — what moved, what it likely means, and how confident to be, with every claim traceable to the underlying data files.

Think of it as a radar system with two independent sensors: one tracks what informed politicians are doing, the other tracks what institutional money is doing right now. When both light up on the same stock, that's the strongest signal.

How a night runs

The pipeline, from close to coffee

Everything runs unattended on a schedule. By the time the operator wakes up, the system has scanned, graded yesterday's calls, recalibrated, and written its report.

22:00

Scan

Pull the day's bars and prints for 212 symbols; detect concentrated volume anomalies against per-symbol baselines.

22:20

Grade

Score every previous prediction against what the market actually did — using a next-day anchor so the system can't credit itself for moves it couldn't have traded.

22:40

Calibrate

Update confidence curves from the full grading history, so a "70%" from the system means 70% — not enthusiasm.

23:00

Interpret

The LLM reads the volume, calibration, and news handoff files and writes the narrative morning report.

07:00

Report

Markdown report + alerts, with a run manifest listing every file the night's analysis touched.

Case study · July 2026

The night I caught my own backtest cheating

Ahab's nightly run makes a call after the market closes, then grades it later. The subtle bug: grading measured each move from that same day's close — a price the system could see but could never have traded. Any overnight gap got silently counted as skill.

The fix was an honest anchor — grade from the next day's open, the first price actually available. Then the hard part: regrade all 4,067 settled predictions and publish whatever fell out.

The experimental momentum lens lost 2.5 points of hit rate — a real chunk of its "edge" was an untradeable gap. But the combined ensemble gained 5.5 points: the old anchor had been unfairly penalizing it. One in six verdicts flipped.

A backtest that flatters you is worse than no backtest at all.

Grading the grader

Does a 70% mean 70%?

Every call Ahab makes carries a confidence score, and every scored call eventually matures into a win or a loss. Plot one against the other and you get the system's honesty curve. Two things are true at once: the ordering is right — higher confidence really does win more often — and the slope is too shallow, so a stated 68% only cashes in at 55%. That gap is measured every night and feeds the recalibration step. I'd rather publish this curve than flatter it.

Under the hood

Engineering it like it matters

CONSTRAINT

$0/month, by design

No paid APIs, no cloud bill: free market-data feeds, SQLite as the event store, launchd for scheduling, and a plan to move LLM inference to a self-hosted open-weights model. Constraints forced better engineering, not worse.

VALIDATION

Walk-forward, out-of-sample

Signals are validated with walk-forward A/B comparisons and out-of-sample blending diagnostics — never fit and scored on the same window. Overlapping outcome windows and unlabeled tails are handled explicitly, not ignored.

CALIBRATION

Confidence that means something

Every prediction carries a probability, and the system tracks whether its 70%s land 70% of the time. Signal snapshots are persisted so calibration can be recomputed retroactively as grading rules improve.

SAFETY

Suggest mode, gated execution

The bot writes reports; a human decides. Automated paper trading is built but deliberately fenced behind a single explicit config gate, with hard rules preventing any code path from loosening it.

DUAL LENS

Baseline vs. experimental

Every symbol gets two independent reads — pure order flow and momentum — reported side by side with the divergence named. When the lenses disagree, that disagreement is the information.

OBSERVABILITY

Every run leaves a manifest

Each nightly scan logs exactly which inputs it read and which reports it wrote. Any number in any report can be traced back to the files that produced it.

Stack, for the technical reader
Python · SQLite event store (detections, executions, volume events, signal snapshots) · Alpaca + yfinance + CCXT market data · pytest · Discord/Telegram alerting · ~40 analysis and backfill scripts · markdown-first reporting. Single-developer, test-covered, no CI/CD theater it doesn't need.

The same skepticism, drawn

Here's a real one, straight from the database. GOOGL over the 17 sessions to July 16 reads like heavy institutional selling: −$39.7B net. But nearly two-thirds of it landed on exactly two calendar days — the Russell reconstitution and the half-year close, the two biggest forced-flow days of the year — and the five most recent sessions quietly flipped to net buying. Strip the plumbing and the story inverts. This is why the scan discounts calendar days instead of trusting raw totals.
